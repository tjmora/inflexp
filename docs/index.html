Abstract

Inflection Expressions, or inflexp for short, are a sequence of characters that define the rules or steps that should be undertaken by an implementing software library in inflecting a base or root word of a natural language. Inflexp has character sequences for prefixation, infixation, suffixation, repetition (of the word or its syllables), sound change (including stress and tone change), sound dropping, supplantation, and others.

This specification is agnostic on how libraries should implement their data structures and method logic. To give an example however, one way to implement inflexp is to define a class Word which main value is of an array type Syllable[] and where each Syllable has some or all of the following properties: preMark: char onset: char[], nucleus: char, coda: char[], stress: bool, tone: char, postMark: char. Then various inflection methods can be defined to manipulate the syllables and their properties.

I. Background

Inflexp originally arose from another open-source natural language project, Halita. Halita is a word-inflection and -deinflection library for Philippine languages like Tagalog. The many native languages of the Philippines are inflected languages. As different Philippine languages have different affixes and different ways of repeating their syllables and root words, a new kind of inflection expression was needed. A simple prefix expression like pag- can only do so much when many other Philippine languages don't only prepend a prefix, but also introduce sound changes and root word transformations along the way.

Early in the development of Halita, it became apparent that its class Word and class Syllable source files can be generalized to work not just with Philippine languages but also with other languages in the world. So those source files were taken out of Halita, moved into a new open source project, Inflexer, and rewritten to reflect their new purpose. This very document serves to standardize the specification of the inflection expression used by Inflexer.

II. Terminology

A character is a symbol or glyph, which can be a letter, a number, an ideogram, a pictogram, a punctuation mark or any other special mark.

A word is a character or a sequence of characters which on its own provides a semantic meaning.

A syllable is a phonological (sound-based) building block of a word. It is comprised at least by a nucleus (which is typically a vowel) with optional onset and final sounds (which are typically consonants).

An onset is any sound, typically a consonant or cluster of consonants, at the start of a syllable before the nucleus. If it is comprised of a cluster of consonants, the first consonant is known as the initial while the last consonant is the medial. The initial can be any consonant while the medial is typically a liquid consonant like <l> or <r> or a semivowel like <w> or <j>.

A nucleus is the central part of a syllable, typically a vowel but can also be a syllabic consonant (a consonant that forms a syllable on its own).

A coda is any sound, typically a consonant or cluster of consonants, in a syllable that comes after the nucleus.

A stress is a an emphasis that may be given to a syllable. Different languages have different methods for stress. It can be increasing the loudness of the syllable, or lengthening the pronunciation of the nucleus, or changing the tone.

A tone is a pitch applied to a syllable. In tonal languages like the Chinese languages, changing the tone of a syllable can change the meaning of the word.

Special marks are any symbol (like the dash <-> and the apostrophe <’>) that a syllable contains. These may not be necessarily important when inflecting the words but may be very important on how the raw syllables are decoded for accurate printing.

III. Inflection Expressions

The table below uses base words that are entirely artificial. As such, the words have no special cases nor irregularities when inflected through inflexp argument-taking methods. Any natural language/orthography-agnostic library should arrive to the same results as shown in the tables below when inflecting the same base word with the same inflexp argument in the corresponding row of each result. Natural language-specific (non-agnotic) libraries, on the other hand, will necessarily consider any irregularity in the words they are taking, with only their default behavior for regular words matching the behaviors show in the tables below.

The base words and resulting words in the tables below are shown syllabified to better show how the words should be represented internally in any implementing library. In reality, implementing libraries should be able to take un-syllabified words and do their own syllabification of them, and output results in a valid orthography without syllabification.

The example words in the tables below use the letters <a>, <e>, <i>, <o>, and <u> exclusively as the nucleus of syllables. Any other letter or cluster of other letters bounding the nucleus are the initial, medial and/or coda. A single letter to the left of a nucleus in a syllable is the syllable's initial. If there are two letters to the left of the nucleus, the first one is the initial and the second one is the medial. The letter or letters to the right of the nucleus in a syllable is/are the coda.

Note: The example words in the tables below avoided using digraphs. That is something the implementing libraries may also need to take into account.

IV. Implementations

Inflexer - An orthography-agnostic JS/TypeScript library that takes inflexp arguments for its inflection methods.

Halita - An inflection and de-inflection JS/TypeScript library for Philippine languages. It extends Inflexer methods for a more accurate inflection and de-inflection of Philippine native words. Halita's own methods can take inflexp arguments.

Inflexer-py - An orthography-agnostic Python 3 library that takes inflexp arguments for its inflection methods.
